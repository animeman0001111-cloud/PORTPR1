Linkage Mechanism

A mechanical arrangement of rigid bodies (links) connected by joints to transmit motion and force from one part of a machine to another in a desired manner.

01

Links

Rigid members that transmit motion and force through the mechanism.

02

Joints

Kinematic pairs — connections between links that allow relative motion (e.g., pin joints, sliding joints).

03

Frame

The fixed link — the stationary part that supports the entire mechanism.

04

Input Link

The driver — the link where motion or force is applied to start the mechanism.

05

Output Link

The follower — the link that produces the required output motion.

Applications

Real-world uses of linkage mechanisms across everyday engineering systems.

Automobile Steering System

Converts steering wheel rotation into precise wheel movement for directional control.

Windshield Wipers

Transform rotary motor motion into oscillatory sweeping motion across the windshield.

Bicycle Pedals & Chains

Transmit human pedalling effort into continuous wheel rotation for forward movement.

Four-Bar Mechanism

Made up of four rigid links connected by four turning pairs (revolute joints) — one of the most common and fundamental linkage types.

Parts of a Four-Bar Mechanism

1. Frame

The stationary fixed link.

2. Input Link (Crank)

The link that receives motion.

3. Coupler Link

Connects the crank and the output link.

4. Output Link (Rocker)

The link that delivers output motion.

Crank-Rocker

One link (the crank) makes a complete rotation while the other (the rocker) oscillates back and forth.

Example: Windshield wiper system

Double Crank

Both links connected to the frame rotate completely, enabling continuous rotation on both sides.

Example: Bicycle chain / engine timing gear

Double Rocker

Both the input and output links only oscillate and do not make full rotations.

Example: Rocking chair, robotic joints

MSBS of Chassis

Understanding linkage mechanisms and volumetric methods together makes engineering calculations more precise and design decisions more effective.

⚙

Linkage Mechanisms

Show how motion is transmitted and transformed (rotary ↔ oscillatory ↔ linear). Allow engineers to derive speed ratios, torque requirements, and force distribution across joints.

📐

Volumetric Methods

Estimate component mass by calculating volume × material density. These mass values feed directly into inertia and load calculations.

🏗

Chassis Impact

Accurate mass estimation prevents overdesign (too heavy) or underdesign (too weak). Ensures load paths are realistic, improving structural efficiency and durability.

⚡

Actuator Impact

Correct torque and speed calculations help select motors that match mechanism demands, preventing energy waste, overheating, or mechanical failure.

"Together, these methods ensure that mass, speed, and torque are derived from geometry, material properties, and kinematics — not guesswork."
